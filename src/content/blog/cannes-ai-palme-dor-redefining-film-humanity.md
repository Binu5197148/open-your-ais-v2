<p>When the news broke on May 1, 2026, that the Cannes Film Festival had awarded its first-ever Palme d&apos;Or to an AI-generated film, my first reaction was a mix of awe and a familiar filmmaker&apos;s dread. For 14 years, I&apos;ve lived and breathed commercial and comedy filmmaking. I&apos;ve directed, I&apos;ve edited, I&apos;ve written. I&apos;ve seen the magic happen with human teams, sweating and laughing, pushing boundaries for clients like Disney, Nestlé, and Kopenhagen. This award, however, feels different. It demands a serious conversation about what we, as creators, actually do.</p>
<p>Many in the industry will see this as the definitive end, a machine usurping the very soul of human artistry. They&apos;ll lament the loss of jobs, the death of craft. I understand that fear. It&apos;s a natural reaction when something so fundamental to our identity is challenged. But from my perspective in São Paulo, running Pichorra Filmes since 2012, and now building AI automation tools, I see this not as an ending, but as a violent, necessary invitation to redefine.</p>

<h2>The Myth of the Autonomous Artist</h2>
<p>Throughout my career, I&apos;ve worn every hat on set: composer, actor, editor, writer, director. From the intimate chaos of a comedy shoot for Ronald Rios Talk Show on Paramount to the meticulous precision required for a Yamaha commercial, every role was about solving a human problem with human ingenuity. We built narratives, evoked emotions, and sometimes, simply made people laugh. That &quot;human touch&quot; was the non-negotiable core.</p>
<p>This Palme d&apos;Or for an AI-generated film doesn&apos;t diminish the value of that human touch. Pelo contrário, it highlights it. An AI doesn&apos;t have a &quot;vision&quot; in the way a human director does. It doesn&apos;t experience heartbreak, joy, or the struggle of bringing an idea to life with limited resources. It generates based on patterns, data, and prompts. The &quot;artistry&quot; of this Cannes winner, I&apos;d argue, lies less in the AI&apos;s inherent creative spark, and more in the human intelligence that designed, trained, and curated that AI.</p>
<p>The output might be astounding, challenging our aesthetic sensibilities, but it doesn&apos;t come from a place of lived experience. It&apos;s not because an algorithm suddenly &quot;understood&quot; tragedy or triumph. It&apos;s because it processed millions of examples of human expressions of tragedy and triumph, and then synthesized them into something new. The credit for the spark of the idea, the initial directive, and the final selection still ultimately traces back to human hands and minds.</p>

<h2>Redefining Authorship, Not Replacing It</h2>
<p>I never wanted to be a programmer. My passion was always for storytelling, for the visceral act of creation. AI, for me, has opened doors that my budget or team size never allowed before. It&apos;s allowed me to explore complex ideas, automate tedious tasks, and even test narrative concepts with unprecedented speed. But it has always been about amplifying *my* capacity, not replacing *my* core creative role.</p>
<p>The Cannes decision forces us to rethink authorship. Is the author the one who wrote the script? The director who shaped the vision? The editor who found the rhythm? Or is it the one who crafted the prompt, fine-tuned the model, and selected the &quot;best&quot; output from an infinite algorithmic stream? This isn&apos;t about AI replacing the writer or director; it&apos;s about adding a new, powerful layer to the creative team.</p>
<p>Think about a high-budget commercial. Even with millions of dollars and dozens of specialists, the human creative director&apos;s fingerprint is paramount. Now, imagine that director with an AI assistant that can generate thousands of visual concepts in minutes, storyboard entire scenes, or even draft initial dialogue variations. The director&apos;s role doesn&apos;t disappear; it evolves into curation, refinement, and injecting that unique, irreplaceable human insight that makes a piece resonate deeply.</p>

<h2>The Human Imperative: Why Our Stories Still Matter</h2>
<p>My work with clients like Carrefour and Starbucks, or the comedic timing required for Jovem Pan, taught me that genuine connection comes from genuine understanding. AI can mimic human emotion, it can even create compelling narratives that pass for human-made. But can it truly connect on a soul level? Can it tap into the shared human experience of yearning, love, loss, without ever having felt them?</p>
<p>My answer, unequivocally, is no. The value of human storytelling doesn&apos;t come from technical perfection; it comes from shared vulnerability, from the messy, beautiful reality of being alive. That&apos;s what I try to bring to Open Your AIs and Verso Diário today. AI can provide the canvas, the paints, even suggest brushstrokes. But the artist who chooses the palette, who imbues the stroke with meaning, that&apos;s still us.</p>
<p>This Cannes award is a siren call, not a death knell. It pushes us to define more clearly what makes our art uniquely human. It&apos;s a challenge to collaborate with these powerful new tools, to understand their capabilities, and to integrate them in a way that amplifies our human stories, rather than diminishes them. The future of film won&apos;t be about humans versus AI. It will be about humans using AI to tell stories that are more powerful, more personal, and more impactful than ever before. We define the art, not the algorithm.</p>
