<p>When I saw the news from CBC about the indie film &quot;As Deep as the Grave&quot; using generative AI to &apos;resurrect&apos; Val Kilmer, portraying him across different ages, my first reaction wasn&apos;t simple. It wasn&apos;t a &apos;yes&apos; or &apos;no&apos; to the technology itself. It was a complex mix of understanding the temptation, acknowledging the potential, and feeling a deep unease about the soul of our craft.</p>
<p>The filmmakers, Coerte and John Voorhees, say they did it &apos;ethically,&apos; collaborating with Kilmer&apos;s children and following SAG-AFTRA guidelines. On paper, it sounds like they checked the boxes. But as someone who has spent 14 years directing, editing, and even acting in productions, from small commercials to larger projects for brands like Disney and Nestlé, I know &apos;ethical&apos; in art goes deeper than a signed contract.</p>

<h2>The Irresistible Lure of the Impossible</h2>
<p>Let&apos;s be honest. For an independent filmmaker, the thought of casting an actor of Val Kilmer&apos;s caliber, especially for a multi-age role, is often an impossible dream. Budgets are tight. Logistics are complex. Every choice on set, from lighting to location, is a negotiation with reality. AI, on the surface, offers a way around these limitations.</p>
<p>I founded Pichorra Filmes in São Paulo in 2012. I&apos;ve been the director, the editor, the writer, the composer—sometimes all at once. I&apos;ve done every role on set because that&apos;s what you do when you want to tell a story and your resources are limited. AI, for me, opened doors that my budget or team size simply couldn&apos;t allow. Tools like Midjourney or Stable Diffusion offer possibilities that were once the exclusive domain of high-budget visual effects houses.</p>
<p>So, I understand the desire to use technology to achieve an ambitious artistic vision. To bring a legendary presence to a screen when traditional means are out of reach. It&apos;s not always about cutting corners. Sometimes, it&apos;s about making a project possible at all.</p>

<h2>The Unquantifiable Human Equation</h2>
<p>But here&apos;s where the unease starts. What makes a performance truly great? Is it just the visual representation of an actor&apos;s face and voice? Or is it the unique, unrepeatable spark of their consciousness, their lived experience, their soul pouring into a role?</p>
<p>Having worked as an actor myself, I know a performance isn&apos;t just a collection of movements or lines. It&apos;s a connection. It&apos;s the way an actor interprets a script, brings a character to life with their subtle gestures, their improvisations, the very energy they bring to the space. It&apos;s an organic, living thing.</p>
<p>This is not a technical problem to be solved with better algorithms. This is about the very essence of human creativity. When I was directing segments for the Ronald Rios Talk Show or producing a program for Jovem Pan, I wasn&apos;t just telling actors where to stand. I was working with their instincts, their reactions, the unforeseen magic they brought to the moment. Can AI truly replicate that? Or does it merely simulate it?</p>

<h2>Beyond Legal Compliance: An Ethical Compass for Artists</h2>
<p>The filmmakers of &quot;As Deep as the Grave&quot; emphasize their compliance with SAG-AFTRA guidelines and family consent. This is a crucial first step, absolutely. We need clear legal frameworks and compensation models to protect artists&apos; rights and legacies in the AI era. But legal compliance, in art, is often the floor, not the ceiling, of ethical responsibility.</p>
<p>The debate isn&apos;t just about whether it&apos;s legal to use AI to recreate a deceased actor. It&apos;s about whether it&apos;s *right* for the art form. It&apos;s about preserving the sanctity of human contribution. It&apos;s not enough to simply recreate a likeness. We have to ask: what is lost in the process? What precedent does it set?</p>
<p>This isn&apos;t just about Val Kilmer. It&apos;s about every actor, every artist, every creator. If we allow technology to fully simulate human presence without truly engaging with human contribution, we risk devaluing the very soul of what we do. It&apos;s a slippery slope toward viewing talent as a commodity, a dataset, rather than a unique, irreplaceable gift.</p>

<h2>AI Amplifies Human Teams. It Does Not Replace Humanity.</h2>
<p>My work with Open Your AIs and Soul Symple is all about using AI to amplify human potential, not to diminish it. I build automation tools because I believe AI can free up creative professionals from repetitive tasks, allowing them to focus more on what only humans can do: imagine, feel, connect, and create truly original work. AI amplifies teams; it does not replace people.</p>
<p>The temptation to use AI to fill what feels like an insurmountable gap in production, whether it&apos;s a specific actor or a complex visual effect, is immense. But we must approach these decisions with profound respect for human contribution. It&apos;s not because AI can do it. It&apos;s because the essence of art requires a human hand, a human heart, and a human soul behind it.</p>
<p>We are not building a future where one person does the work of ten. We are building a future where one human, amplified by intelligent tools, can achieve what was once impossible, but always with their unique vision and humanity at the core. The &apos;digital resurrection&apos; of an actor, even with consent, forces us to confront this question head-on. Our answer must always prioritize the human element that breathes life into stories.</p>

<p>We are filmmakers, not just technicians. Our craft is about capturing and conveying the human experience. AI is a powerful brush, but the canvas, the vision, and the soul must always remain profoundly, unyieldingly human.</p>
